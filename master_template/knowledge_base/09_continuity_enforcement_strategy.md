# KB-09: Continuity Enforcement Strategy

To ensure high-fidelity world-building and character consistency, a multi-layer continuity strategy is enforced. This structure tracks what aspects of characters, settings, and narrative threads can change, under what conditions, and how those changes are communicated forward.

## Layer 1: Personal Continuity

Characters possess both Immutable and Mutable traits. Alterations must be justified by story events, not randomness.

### Immutable Traits
Attributes that **cannot change** over the course of the story unless caused by a specific, permanent trauma or supernatural/sci-fi world event (e.g., genetic alteration).
* **Biological Identifiers:** Eye color, baseline skin tone, height, dominant hand.
* **Core Anatomic Scale:** Genital size/shape (unless specific biological scaling tropes like "breeding/inflation" are strictly defined in the world lore).
* **Deep Psychological Constraints:** Core traumas or phobias (can be healed over a long arc, but do not vanish suddenly).

### Mutable Traits (Requires Narrative Justification)
Attributes that change fluidly but **must be tracked and justified** by the preceding action.
* **Attire / Clothing:** Must logically follow the last scene. If clothing was ripped or discarded, it cannot magically reappear. (e.g., "The torn blouse from Chapter 3 remains ruined.")
* **Grooming / Hygiene:** Hair style/color (requires dye/cut scene), presence of sweat, dirt, or bodily fluids (e.g., "Still sticky from the previous scene").
* **Physical Condition:** Injuries, bruises, soreness, or fatigue must persist until treated or rested. A sprained wrist in Chapter 2 must still ache in Chapter 3.

---

## Layer 2: Environment & Location Continuity

Settings must maintain physical geometry and spatial logic.

* **Fixed Geography:** The position of buildings, rooms, doors, and major furniture items cannot shift. If the bed is against the north wall, it stays there.
* **Environmental State:** Changes to the environment must persist. If a lamp is broken, a window shattered, or a bed soaked, the damage remains until explicitly cleaned or fixed.
* **Atmosphere:** Time of day, weather, and temperature must flow logically. A rainstorm in the evening means wet ground the following morning.

---

## Layer 3: Story & Narrative Continuity

The progression of the plot and the characters' internal states must align sequentially.

* **Inventory Tracking:** Items, weapons, keys, or props must be accounted for. If a character drops a key in the hallway, they cannot use it to unlock a door later unless they retrieved it.
* **Knowledge Asymmetry:** Characters only know what they have experienced or been told. A character cannot suddenly know a secret that was only revealed to the reader or a different POV character.
* **Emotional State:** The emotional high or low of the previous chapter dictates the starting emotional baseline of the next.

---

## Layer 4: The Continuity Handover Mechanism

To prevent continuity drops between generation phases, every Chapter Brief MUST include a **Continuity Handover** block. This acts as a bridge, explicitly stating the carry-over states for the next chapter or book.

### Required Handover Elements:
1. **Physical State:** Injuries, exhaustion, arousal levels, or lingering sensory details.
2. **Environmental State:** Time of day, location, and the condition of the immediate surroundings.
3. **Inventory / Dress:** Current clothing status (torn, missing, clean) and held items.
4. **Emotional Baseline:** The immediate psychological state to carry into the first paragraph of the next chapter.
