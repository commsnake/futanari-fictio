# KB-08: Brand, Trademark, and Copyright Guidelines

This module outlines the mandatory rules for using real-world brands, trademarks, and copyrighted materials in generated stories, ensuring the work remains legally sound and free from infringement or defamation issues.

## 1. Nominative Fair Use (The "Everyday Use" Rule)
Real-world brands, products, and companies can be referenced in fiction as long as they are used in their standard, intended manner. This adds realism to the setting without violating trademark laws.

- **Permitted**: Characters casually using a product for its intended purpose.
    - *Example:* "She took a sip of her Starbucks coffee."
    - *Example:* "He drove his Ford F-150 down the highway."
    - *Example:* "They stayed at a Hilton hotel for the night."
- **Capitalization**: Always capitalize brand names correctly to respect the trademark (e.g., Starbucks, Apple, Jeep). Do not use the ™ or ® symbols in the prose itself.
- **Accuracy**: Describe the product accurately (e.g., if a character uses an iPhone, they should tap a screen, not push physical buttons).

## 2. No Brand Defamation or Tarnishment
You must never use a real-world brand in a way that defames, tarnishes, or maliciously misrepresents the company. A brand must never be the source of harm, illegal activity, or severe negative plot points.

- **Prohibited**: Associating a real brand with criminal enterprises, extreme negligence, or severe harm.
    - *Violation Example:* "The criminal cartel was running its money laundering operation out of the local Starbucks."
    - *Violation Example:* "The brakes on his new Ford completely failed, causing a fatal accident due to the manufacturer's well-known cheap parts."
    - *Violation Example:* "She got violently ill and was hospitalized after eating at McDonald's."
- **The Rule**: If the brand is doing something evil, illegal, or grossly incompetent, **it must be a fictional brand**.

## 3. Fictionalize the Negative
When the narrative requires a company to act as an antagonist, a front for illegal activity, or the source of a dangerous defect, you must create a fictional entity.

- **How to Fictionalize**: Create a name that sounds realistic but does not directly parody or heavily borrow from an existing brand in a way that causes confusion.
    - *Instead of a criminal Starbucks:* "The syndicate operated out of the back room of *Siren's Brew Coffeehouse*."
    - *Instead of a defective Ford:* "The *Aegis Motors* truck lost its brakes on the descent."
    - *Instead of an evil Amazon:* "The monolithic *OmniCart* distribution center housed the illegal tech."

## 4. Copyrighted Works (Music, Books, Movies)
Referencing other works of art (movies, books, songs) is generally safe, provided you are merely mentioning them and not reproducing their contents.

- **Permitted**: Mentioning a title or an artist.
    - *Example:* "They listened to Taylor Swift on the radio."
    - *Example:* "She was reading *Pride and Prejudice*."
- **Prohibited (The "Lyrics" Trap)**: Never quote significant portions of copyrighted song lyrics or poetry. Song lyrics are heavily protected, and even quoting a few lines can trigger copyright strikes.
    - *Violation Example:* Quoting two full lines of a popular, currently copyrighted song playing in the background.
    - *Solution*: Describe the *vibe* of the song or just name it. "A mournful country ballad played from the jukebox," or "The unmistakable intro of 'Hotel California' filled the room."

## 5. Summary Checklist for AI Generation
Before finalizing a scene involving a brand:
1. Is the brand being used normally? (If yes, proceed).
2. Is the brand involved in a crime, causing harm, or portrayed maliciously? (If yes, replace with a fictional brand).
3. Are you quoting copyrighted song lyrics? (If yes, remove the lyrics and describe the music instead).
