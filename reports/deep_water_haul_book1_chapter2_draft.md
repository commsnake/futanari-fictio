# Chapter 2: The First Swell

The stainless steel of the galley counter was the only thing keeping Leo upright. His knuckles were white, tendons straining against the thin, clammy skin of his hands as he gripped the edge of the sink. Beneath his feet, the deck pitched violently. The sixty-foot *Maiden’s Mast* didn’t just ride the waves; she slammed into them, shuddering with the impact before dropping into the trough on the other side. Every drop sent Leo’s stomach into a sickening freefall.

He swallowed hard, fighting the rising bile in his throat. The heat in the galley was sweltering, an oppressive combination of the dual diesel stoves burning on high and the ambient heat bleeding through the bulkhead from the engine room directly below. The roar of the massive marine engines was a constant, deafening physical pressure, vibrating up through the thick rubber matting and straight into his bones.

Leo was sweating through his cheap, thin grey hoodie. The cotton clung to his narrow back, slick with fear and exertion. He hadn't slept since boarding. The noise of his bunk, pressed right up against the steel hull, had made sure of that. Now, less than six hours out of port, he was paying the price. He was a ninety-five-pound runaway who had never been on a boat larger than a ferry, and the ocean was tearing him apart.

He had to focus. The contract. *Ten thousand dollars.* He just needed to survive long enough to jump ship at the next port.

He forced his eyes to focus on the whiteboard bolted to the wall above the sink. The Captain’s meal requirements were scrawled in harsh, aggressive black marker. *Breakfast: Eggs, Bacon, Toast, Coffee (Black). Lunch: Heavy Stew, Bread. Dinner: Steak, Potatoes, Greens.*

Right now, he needed to make the stew. He grabbed the largest pot he could find, hauling its heavy iron frame onto the stove and locking it into the steel railing that kept the cookware from flying across the room during heavy seas. He turned on the faucet, wincing as the water sputtered and splashed onto his jeans before running clear. He filled the pot, his arms shaking under the weight.

Chopping the vegetables was a nightmare. He had to brace his hips against the counter, standing with his legs spread wide to maintain balance. The knife felt heavy and unwieldy in his shaking hands. Every time the boat rolled, the blade slipped, skittering across the cutting board. He managed to dice the onions, potatoes, and carrots into uneven chunks, sweeping them into the pot before turning his attention to the meat.

The heavy, bloody slabs of beef smelled overwhelmingly raw in the close heat of the galley. He gagged, turning his head away, trying to breathe exclusively through his mouth. The scent mingled with the old grease coating the hood above the stove, the diesel fumes leaking from the engine room, and the sharp, salty tang of the sea air blowing in through the open ventilation hatch. It was a nauseating cocktail.

He tossed the meat into the pot, turned up the heat, and stepped back, gasping for air. The boat hit another swell, throwing him hard against the edge of the counter. His hip bone cracked against the metal, sending a sharp spike of pain radiating down his leg.

"Dammit," he hissed through his teeth, rubbing his side.

Hours blurred into a hazy, agonizing struggle. He managed to add the thickener, stirring the heavy stew until it bubbled thickly. He had burned the back of his hand on the hot edge of the pot, a sharp, angry red line forming across his knuckles, but he couldn't stop. He had to keep moving. He had to prove he was useful, or at least, not entirely useless.

Finally, the stew was done. He killed the heat, leaning heavily against the counter, his chest heaving. He wiped his sweaty forehead with the back of his arm, leaving a smear of grease across his pale skin. He looked at the bubbling pot, feeling a brief, fragile sense of accomplishment. He had done it. He had survived his first task.

The heavy metal door of the galley swung open with a violent crash.

Leo jumped, spinning around.

Inez Rivera, Deckhand A, filled the doorway. She was a mountain of a woman, clad in heavy, yellow weather gear that was completely soaked with salt spray. Her dark hair was plastered to her skull, and her face was streaked with fish blood and grime. She looked exhausted, her heavy shoulders slumped, but her dark eyes were sharp and predatory. She smelled strongly of ozone, sweat, and the raw, metallic scent of the catch.

She stepped into the galley, pulling off her heavy rubber gloves and tossing them onto the counter with a wet slap. "Food. Now."

Her voice was harsh, demanding, completely lacking in patience.

Leo’s heart slammed against his ribs. He scrambled for a bowl, his hands shaking so badly he nearly dropped it. He grabbed the heavy metal ladle, plunging it into the pot and scooping up a generous portion of the thick, hot stew. He poured it into the bowl, trying to keep his hands steady.

He turned, holding the bowl out to her. "Here."

Inez reached for the bowl, her large hand extending toward him.

At that exact moment, the *Maiden’s Mast* hit a massive trough. The deck dropped entirely out from under Leo.

He lost his footing on the greasy floor grate. His worn sneakers found no purchase. He flailed wildly, his arms shooting out to catch himself.

The bowl flew from his grasp.

Time seemed to slow down. The hot, heavy stew launched through the air, splattering violently across the deck. A large splash of the boiling liquid hit Inez’s heavy rubber boots, sizzling slightly against the wet material.

Dead silence fell over the galley, broken only by the relentless, deafening rumble of the engines below.

Leo hit the deck hard, his knees bruising against the metal grates. He scrambled backward, his eyes wide with horror as he looked at the mess on the floor. The stew was everywhere, thick and greasy, covering the deck and Inez's boots.

Inez didn't move. She stood perfectly still, looking down at the spilled food. Then, slowly, she lifted her head and looked at Leo.

The exhaustion in her eyes vanished entirely. It was replaced by a dark, volatile anger. Her jaw clenched, the muscles ticking visibly beneath her skin. She took a slow, deliberate step forward, her heavy boots crushing the potatoes and meat into the grates.

"I... I'm sorry," Leo stammered, his voice cracking. He tried to scramble further back, but he hit the unyielding stainless steel of the counter. He was cornered. "The boat, it... it pitched. I slipped."

Inez didn't respond. She took another step, looming over him. She was massive, terrifying, completely filling his field of vision. The air in the galley suddenly felt incredibly thin.

She stopped right in front of him, looking down with an expression of pure disdain. She reached down, her large hand grabbing the collar of his hoodie. With a single, brutal movement, she hauled him to his feet.

Leo gasped, his toes barely touching the ground. He grabbed at her arm, trying to pry her fingers loose, but her grip was like iron.

"You think this is a joke, Super-Greenhorn?" she snarled, her breath hot against his face. "You think you can just waste our food? Make a mess on my deck?"

"No, I didn't mean to—"

"Shut up," she snapped, cutting him off. Her dark eyes bored into his, utterly dominant. "You signed the contract. You know the rules. You upset the crew, you put it right."

Leo's stomach dropped. The memory of the contract flashed through his mind, the heavy black ink spelling out his doom. *Absolute, unquestioning physical submission.*

Inez shoved him hard against the counter, her massive body pressing against his. She leaned in close, her voice dropping to a low, dangerous growl. "You just made a very expensive mistake, boy. And you're going to pay for it."
