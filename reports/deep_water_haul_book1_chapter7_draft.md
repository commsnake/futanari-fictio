# Chapter 7: The "Money" Mishap

The aft deck of the *Maiden’s Mast* was a slaughterhouse.

The fading afternoon light was rapidly replaced by the harsh, blinding glare of the industrial halogens bolted to the wheelhouse. Beneath the lights, the ocean boiled. The crew had hit a massive school of yellowfin, and the vessel was alive with a frantic, violent energy that Leo had never experienced.

The deafening scream of the hydraulic winches tore through the roar of the wind, hauling thousands of pounds of thrashing muscle and silver scales out of the freezing Atlantic. The deck was slick with sea foam, crushed ice, and the dark, metallic sheen of fresh blood.

The Futanari crew were in their absolute element. They moved with a brutal, terrifying efficiency, their massive bodies coordinating the chaos with silent, practiced precision. Inez was working the heavy gaff, driving the sharp steel hook into the massive fish as they breached, her thick arms corded with immense exertion.

And orchestrating it all was Ox.

The Deck Boss was a force of nature, her voice booming over the mechanical din, directing the lines, calling the tensions, and physically manhandling the massive catch into the holding pens. She was covered in salt spray and blood, her chest heaving, an apex predator engaged in a mass slaughter.

Leo was running on pure adrenaline. He dashed between the sweltering galley and the freezing deck, carrying heavy thermoses of black coffee and thick, meat-heavy sandwiches wrapped in foil. The crew didn't stop to eat; they snatched the food from his hands, devouring it in seconds while keeping one hand on the lines.

He stepped out of the galley door, a fresh, steaming thermos clutched to his chest. The wind immediately cut through his sweat-soaked hoodie, but he ignored it. He was focused on Ox. He wanted to be useful. He wanted that brief, heavy nod of approval.

He stepped onto the bloody deck, his sneakers slipping slightly on the slick steel.

"Boss!" Leo yelled, holding the thermos up.

Ox didn't hear him. She was focused on a massive, thrashing yellowfin that Inez was struggling to pull over the railing.

"Hold the line!" Inez shrieked, her boots sliding backward on the bloody deck as the fish made a violent, desperate run back toward the deep.

The main hydraulic line connected to the winch suddenly spiked in tension. The thick, steel-woven cable snapped taut with a sound like a gunshot, vibrating with lethal, unimaginable force.

Leo, trying to get out of the way of Inez's sliding boots, stepped backward.

He stepped directly into the path of the tension line.

"Greenhorn! Move!" Inez screamed, her voice cracking with sudden terror.

Leo froze. His brain couldn't process the geometry of the deck fast enough. He looked at the vibrating cable, completely unaware that if the fish breached again, the line would snap across the deck with enough force to cut him in half.

Ox saw the geometry instantly.

She dropped her gaff. The heavy steel tool clattered uselessly against the railing.

She lunged across the slick, blood-covered deck with a speed that defied her massive size. She didn't yell. She didn't warn him.

She slammed into Leo like a runaway freight train.

The impact knocked the wind entirely out of his lungs. Leo was thrown backward, his feet leaving the deck completely as Ox tackled him.

Less than a fraction of a second later, the massive yellowfin breached. The hydraulic line whipped violently across the exact space where Leo's head had just been, the steel cable singing a lethal, high-pitched note in the freezing air.

Leo hit the steel deck hard. The back of his skull bounced off the metal, sending a blinding flash of white light behind his eyes.

The thermos shattered instantly between them. Boiling hot coffee exploded outward, mixing with the freezing fish blood and sea foam coating the deck.

For a terrifying second, the chaotic noise of the deck seems to stop.

Ox hauled herself up, her face inches from Leo's, absolutely furious.

The adrenaline of the near-death experience hadn't faded; it had transmuted instantly into a dark, violent, possessive rage. She didn't yell. She didn't bark a command. Her face was inches from his, her dark eyes entirely dilated, an expression of pure, unadulterated dominance.

She grabbed the front of his soaked, blood-stained hoodie. With a single, brutal heave, she hauled him up off the deck, dragging him to his feet.

Leo swayed, his knees buckling, but Ox held him upright effortlessly, her massive fist bunched tightly in the fabric against his chest.

"You stupid, useless little rat," Ox snarled, her voice a low, vibrating rumble that felt like an earthquake in his chest. "You almost got your head taken off."

"I... I'm sorry," Leo choked out, tasting blood and coffee. "I didn't see the line. I was just trying to bring the coffee—"

"Shut up," Ox hissed, the command cutting him off like a knife.

She leaned in, her heavy chest pressing hard against him. The physical authority was absolute, completely suffocating. The Futanari biology he had been fantasizing about for days was suddenly very real, very present, and completely furious.

"You broke the rhythm of the haul," Ox said, her dark eyes burning into his. "You caused a disruption. You stepped out of line."

Leo's heart hammered against his ribs. The words from the fish hold echoed in his mind. The contract. *Upset the crew, put it right.*

He looked at her, his terror entirely mixed with that deep, confusing, submissive craving. He hadn't just almost died; he had angered the only authority figure that mattered to him anymore.

"I'm sorry, Boss," Leo whispered, his voice trembling, offering no excuses, no resistance.

Ox stared at him for a long, heavy moment. The rage in her eyes shifted, darkening into something much more dangerous. The protective instinct had successfully kept him alive, but the possessive instinct demanded compensation.

"You owe the ship, greenhorn," Ox rumbled, her voice dropping an octave.

She didn't let him go. She tightened her grip on his hoodie, turned on her heel, and began dragging him off the bloody aft deck, heading straight toward the narrow door of the crew quarters.

Leo stumbled behind her, slipping on the slick metal, his heart racing with a terrifying, absolute anticipation. The 'money' mishap had sealed his fate for the night.
