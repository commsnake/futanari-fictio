# Chapter 3: The Contract Enforced

Inez had Leo pinned flush against the stainless steel sink, her heavy forearm pressing tight across his collarbone. The air in the galley was thick with the smell of spilled stew and diesel exhaust, but all Leo could focus on was the dark, unyielding fury in the deckhand's eyes. His heart hammered a frantic rhythm against his ribs. He was ninety-five pounds of pure panic, trapped by a woman who could likely crush his windpipe without breaking a sweat.

"You just made a very expensive mistake, boy," Inez growled, the muscles in her thick neck corded with tension. "And you're going to pay for it."

Before Leo could stammer out another useless apology, the heavy metal door of the galley swung open. It hit the bulkhead with a sharp, echoing *clang*.

Inez didn't flinch, but her grip on Leo loosened by a fraction of an inch.

Leo dragged his gaze toward the door.

"Ox" Boudreaux, the Deck Boss, filled the frame. She was a monolith of a woman, broader across the shoulders than anyone Leo had ever seen, wrapped in heavy, stained canvas work gear. Her expression was utterly unreadable, a stoic mask carved from weathered stone. She stepped into the sweltering galley, her heavy boots coming to a slow stop just at the edge of the spilled stew.

She surveyed the scene with cold, calculating efficiency. The ruined meal on the deck. The trembling, pale boy cornered against the sink. The furious deckhand holding him there.

The hierarchy in the room shifted instantly. The raw, volatile aggression bleeding off Inez seemed to temper, reining itself in under the sheer, immovable authority radiating from the Deck Boss.

"What happened here?" Ox asked. Her voice wasn't loud. It was a low, resonant rumble that easily cut through the deafening vibration of the marine engines below them.

"Greenhorn dropped my food," Inez snapped, not taking her eyes off Leo. "Wasted the catch. Splattered my boots."

Ox slowly turned her gaze to Leo. The coldness in her eyes sent a fresh spike of terror straight through his gut. It wasn't the hot, predatory anger of Inez; it was the cold, mechanical assessment of a problem that needed fixing.

"Is that right, boy?" Ox asked.

Leo swallowed hard, his throat dry as bone. "The boat," he croaked, his voice cracking. "The boat pitched. I slipped. The floor is greasy, and I couldn't catch my balance—"

"The ocean doesn't care about excuses," Ox cut him off smoothly. Her tone was absolute. "The ocean doesn't care if you slip. The ocean only cares about what gets done."

She took a slow, heavy step forward.

Leo shrank back as far as the counter would allow, his knuckles white where he gripped the edge of the steel. "I'll clean it up," he said quickly, his words stumbling over each other. "I'll make another pot right now. I swear."

Ox stopped directly in front of him, looming over him like a storm front. "You'll clean it up," she agreed, her voice dropping lower. "But first, you put it right."

Leo's breath hitched. *Put it right.* The words echoed in his head, pulling the memory of the Articles of Employment from the dark corners of his panic. The thick black ink. The draconian clauses he had skimmed in his desperation to escape the mainland.

"I don't—" Leo started, but the words died in his throat.

Ox didn't wait for him to finish. She reached out with terrifying speed. Her massive hand clamped down on the collar of his cheap grey hoodie.

With a single, effortless heave, she hauled him away from the counter, pulling him entirely out of Inez's grip.

Leo gasped as his feet left the floor for a split second. He grabbed frantically at her thick wrist, his fingers slipping uselessly against the rough canvas of her sleeve. "Wait!" he yelled, sheer panic seizing him.

Ox ignored him. She turned on her heel and marched out of the galley, dragging him behind her like a sack of unwanted bait.

"No, wait, let me go!" Leo shouted, his sneakers skidding wildly over the greasy metal floor grates as he tried to find purchase.

It was utterly useless. The physical disparity between them was absolute. He was a twig caught in a riptide. Ox didn't even seem to notice his resistance; her stride never broke, her pace never slowed.

They hit the narrow, dimly lit corridor outside the galley. The air here was cooler, but the vibration of the engines was even stronger. Leo stumbled, his knees knocking against the steel bulkhead as Ox hauled him around a tight corner.

He glanced back over his shoulder. Inez was following them. The deckhand walked silently, a dark, imposing shadow trailing right on their heels. Her face was set in a hard, expectant line.

"Where are you taking me?" Leo demanded, his voice pitching high with terror. "I said I'd clean it!"

Ox didn't answer. She dragged him further down the corridor, deeper into the bowels of the ship. The noise of the engines shifted to a dull, rhythmic thumping, replaced by the aggressive, high-pitched whine of massive industrial compressors.

They reached a heavy, insulated steel door at the end of the hall. The words *FISH HOLD - AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY* were stenciled across the metal in faded yellow paint.

Ox stopped, her grip on his collar tightening, choking him slightly. With her free hand, she reached out and grabbed the heavy iron latch mechanism on the door. She threw it open with a loud, metallic *clack*.

She pulled the door open, the heavy rubber seals groaning as they broke contact.

A blast of freezing, hyper-chilled air rolled out of the darkness, hitting Leo like a physical blow. The smell was overpowering—a sharp, stinging mix of ammonia, old blood, and frozen salt.

Before Leo could brace himself, Ox twisted her grip and shoved him hard.

He went flying through the doorway. His feet tangled together, and he crashed hard onto the floor of the hold.

The metal grating beneath him was coated in a thin layer of frost. The cold bit instantly through his thin jeans, stinging the bruises on his knees and hip. He gasped, the freezing air burning his lungs.

He scrambled onto his hands and knees, scrambling backward frantically until his spine hit something solid. He glanced over his shoulder. A stack of empty, heavy-duty plastic fish crates.

He looked back toward the open doorway just as the harsh, industrial fluorescent lights inside the hold flickered on with an aggressive electrical hum.

The space was cavernous, a stark, terrifying cathedral of reinforced steel and frost.

Ox stepped into the hold, her heavy boots crunching softly on the frozen grating. Inez followed, stepping in right beside the Deck Boss.

Ox reached back, grabbing the handle of the heavy insulated door. She pulled it shut.

The heavy steel slammed into the frame. The latch clicked loudly into place, sealing them in.

The finality of the sound echoed in the freezing space. There was no handle on the inside of the door.

Leo was completely trapped.

He pushed his back harder against the plastic crates, pulling his knees up to his chest, shivering violently. He looked up at the two massive women looming over him in the stark, unforgiving light.

Ox looked down at him, her expression completely void of pity. "You signed a contract, Super-Greenhorn," she said, her voice echoing off the frozen steel walls. "You upset the crew. Now, you put it right."

Leo's eyes darted between Ox and Inez. The absolute physical authority they held over him was suffocating. There was nowhere to run. There was no one to hear him. The ocean had swallowed him whole, and the contract he had so blindly signed was about to be enforced.
