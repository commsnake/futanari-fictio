# Book 1, Prelude: The Contract in the Dark

The door was cheap composite wood, swelling with humidity and peeling at the bottom edge. It offered exactly zero protection.

Leo pressed his spine flush against it anyway. He dug the rubber soles of his worn sneakers into the cracked linoleum floor, locking his knees, turning his own ninety-five-pound, underfed frame into a barricade. It wouldn’t stop them. He knew that. If the men in the hallway wanted in, the hinges would rip out of the dry rot on the first kick. But the physical act of bracing himself, of holding off the world by sheer mechanical friction, was the only thing keeping his lungs working.

"I’m telling you, the little rat is on this floor."

The voice bled through the wood, thick with a mainland Jersey accent and heavy with malice. Heavy boots scuffed against the hallway runner.

Leo stopped breathing. He clamped a hand over his mouth, tasting stale sweat and rust from a split lip he’d caught three days ago. Fifteen thousand dollars. It had started as a three-grand front to get a roof over his head after aging out of the system, but the interest compounded weekly, violently. They didn't want the money anymore. They wanted an example.

"Check 4B. If he ain't there, we tear apart the roof."

The heavy footsteps moved away, thumping down the hall toward the stairwell. Leo didn't move. He waited until the silence in the corridor settled into the low, oppressive hum of the building's failing boiler. Slowly, his lungs demanded air. He let it out in a shaky, thin exhale, his chest trembling violently under his thin t-shirt. He slumped down, his ass hitting the linoleum, but he kept his back pressed hard against the door.

He dug into his pocket with a shaking hand and pulled out his cracked smartphone.

The harsh blue light illuminated his cramped space—a moldy mattress on the floor, a single duffel bag holding everything he owned, and a window looking out onto a brick wall and a rusted fire escape. The air tasted of stale beer and fear.

His thumb hovered over his banking app. He didn't need to open it. Zero dollars. Zero cents. The gang debt was a literal death sentence, and the executioners were currently pacing the hallway.

He swiped over to his email.

The screen displayed a reply he’d received three hours ago. Desperation had made him apply to anything, everything, offering cash in hand. He’d found an ad buried on a maritime logistics board, practically screaming for a Galley Boy on a vessel departing out of Barnegat Light.

The text of the original ad glowed starkly through the spiderweb cracks of his screen:
*IMMEDIATE DEPARTURE. ASTOR LONGLINER (F-DESIGNATION). MASSIVE CALORIC PREP REQUIRED. SUPER-GREENHORNS ACCEPTED. TEN THOUSAND DOLLAR SIGN-ON BONUS, CASH IN HAND AT DOCK.*

Ten grand. It was enough to pay down the principal of his debt and buy him a bus ticket to the Midwest. It was enough to vanish.

He’d clicked apply, expecting to be ignored. Instead, the reply had come back almost instantly, carrying a PDF attachment.

*Articles of Maritime Employment.*

Leo opened the document. He skimmed the first section. Words blurred together under the adrenaline pounding in his ears. *High-capacity dry storage… reinforcement maintenance… caloric prep for Futanari biology…* His eyes slid right past the biological designation, his brain refusing to process anything that wasn't a dollar sign. He assumed it was just some esoteric fishing term. A brand of engine. A specific type of tuna. It didn't matter. What mattered was the money.

But as he scrolled down, the legalese shifted. It didn't look like a labor contract anymore. It looked like a trap.

His thumb froze over the screen. He zoomed in on Section IV.

*The "Put it Right" Addendum.*

Leo squinted, reading the glowing text in the pitch-black room.

*...By signing these Articles, the Employee explicitly consents to the F-Designation Discipline Protocol... any professional or social friction is to be "put right" through physical settlement...*

He read it again. And then a third time.

"What the hell is this?" he whispered to the empty room.

He kept scrolling, his stomach dropping. *Free Use Agreement.* *Physical Enforcement.* *Breeding Legacy.* It was written with cold, institutional detachment, explicitly outlining that the signee was surrendering absolute physical autonomy to the crew. If he messed up the cooking, if he talked back, if he so much as annoyed a deckhand, they had the contractual right to use his body to settle the debt.

He stared at the section detailing the "Visual Safeword." A mandatory, unbroken double-tap to a superior's dominant shoulder because the engine noise would drown out a voice.

"They surely can't mean..." Leo muttered, shaking his head. "This is insane."

He nearly dropped the phone. It had to be a joke. Some archaic maritime hazing ritual to scare off weak recruits. People didn't write contracts like this in the real world. You couldn't just legally bind someone to physical submission on a boat. It was absurd. It was illegal.

A heavy slam echoed from the floor above him. Dust drifted down from the ceiling. They were tearing apart the fifth-floor rooms. They would be back down here in minutes.

Leo looked at the cracked screen. The absurdity of the contract warred with the terrifying, immediate reality of the heavy boots upstairs. The contract was a piece of paper. The men upstairs were going to break his legs and throw him in the river.

His foster-kid survival instincts, honed over years of bouncing between bad situations, kicked in. He rationalized it with desperate, flawless logic: He just needed the sign-on bonus. He would meet them at the docks, sign the digital copy to prove he was serious, take the ten grand in cash, and then run. He wouldn't even step foot on the boat. He'd take the money, pay the gang, and disappear.

They couldn't enforce a "Free Use" contract if he was halfway to Ohio on a Greyhound bus.

Another crash from the hallway. Closer this time.

"Hey! Open the damn door!"

Leo jumped. He didn't think anymore. With a trembling index finger, he scrolled to the bottom of the glowing document. He tapped the signature box, hastily scribbling his name with a sweaty fingertip.

He hit reply. He attached the signed document. He pressed send.

The email swooshed away into the digital ether. It sat in his sent folder, a ticking time bomb he foolishly believed he could defuse.

Leo shoved the phone into his pocket. He scrambled away from the door, grabbing the handle of his single, battered duffel bag. He didn't look back at the room. He slid the rusted window open, the cold coastal air hitting his face like a slap, and slipped out onto the fire escape just as the doorknob behind him began to violently rattle.

He was running. As always, he was running, but this time it was towards the *Maiden's Mast*.
