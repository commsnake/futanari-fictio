# Book 1, Chapter 1: Boarding, The Bunk, & The Safeword

The Barnegat Light docks smelled like diesel and dead things.

Leo stood at the edge of the concrete pier, the harsh bite of the early morning coastal wind whipping his shaggy brown hair into his eyes. He pulled his thin jacket tighter, clutching his single, battered duffel bag against his chest like a shield. The sky above the Atlantic was a bruised, bleeding gray, offering no warmth.

Before him sat the *Maiden's Mast*.

It was a sixty-foot Astor Longliner, a sheer wall of rust-streaked, ocean-battered steel. It didn't look like a fishing boat; it looked like a floating refinery. Thick, heavy-duty hydraulic lines snaked across the aft deck, leading to massive, industrial winches capable of hauling thousands of pounds of dead weight from the deep. The sodium lights strung along the rigging cut fiercely through the morning fog, casting long, menacing shadows across the slick deck.

Leo swallowed hard. His mouth was dry.

Fifteen thousand dollars. The number echoed in his head, matching the violent, irregular rhythm of his heart. The gang debt was a loaded gun pointed at the back of his head. He had the digital copy of the signed contract sitting in his phone. Ten grand sign-on, the ad had promised. Cash in hand. He just had to get on the boat, find whoever was in charge, take the envelope, and find a way back off before they cast the lines.

He gripped the strap of his duffel. *Get the money. Get off the boat.*

He stepped off the concrete and onto the heavy wooden gangway. The incline was steep, the wood slick with sea spray and oil. He scrambled up, his worn sneakers slipping slightly, before dropping down onto the main deck.

The vibration hit him instantly. The deck plates thrummed beneath his feet, a deep, bone-rattling pulse emanating from deep within the steel hull. It wasn't the rattle of an old engine; it was the steady, terrifying purr of something massive and incredibly powerful holding itself in check. He looked around. Everything on the deck seemed oversized. The cleats were thicker, the reinforced framing along the gunwales looked like it was built to withstand artillery fire, not just waves.

"You're late."

The voice was quiet, clipped, and devoid of any warmth. It cut cleanly through the ambient noise of the harbor and the hum of the ship.

Leo spun around, his hand instinctively tightening into a fist on his bag.

A figure stepped out from the deep shadows beneath the upper wheelhouse overhang. Leo had to tilt his head up. Way up.

She was easily six foot one, maybe taller in her heavy-duty rubber deck boots. She wore thick, dark canvas work pants and a tight, black thermal shirt that did nothing to hide the severe, coiled musculature of her shoulders and arms. Her jet-black hair was scraped back into a severe, utilitarian bun, exposing sharp, aristocratic cheekbones and a precise, pale surgical scar that ran along her strong jawline.

Leo stared. He had expected some grizzled old salt with a missing finger and a bad attitude. He hadn't expected a woman built like a predatory Olympian.

He masked his immediate physical intimidation the only way he knew how: with thin, defensive sarcasm.

"Sorry," Leo snarked, shifting his weight and refusing to meet her dark, analytical eyes. "Traffic was murder. Actually, I thought I was supposed to be meeting the Captain."

The woman stopped a few feet from him. She didn't blink. She didn't bristle at the attitude. Instead, a slow, terrifyingly cold smirk pulled at the corner of her scarred jaw.

"Captain ain't got time for the likes of you," she said, her voice dropping an octave, carrying a chilling intensity that immediately sucked the bravado out of Leo's lungs. "You deal with me. I report to the Captain. Understand?"

Leo swallowed the lump in his throat. The sarcasm evaporated. He nodded once, a quick, jerky motion.

"I'm Morgan," she said, her eyes slowly tracking down his underfed frame, lingering with blatant, clinical judgment on his cheap street clothes and the way he held his bag. "First Mate. And you are the new Super-Greenhorn."

"Leo," he corrected quietly. "My name is Leo."

"Your name is whatever I tell you it is when we are on the clock, Leo," Morgan replied. The lack of inflection made it worse. It wasn't a threat; it was a statement of maritime law. "Follow me. We need to process your paperwork before the Old Man calls for cast-off."

She turned sharply on her heel, expecting unquestioning obedience. Leo hesitated for a fraction of a second, his flight instinct screaming at him to turn around and run back down the gangway. But he thought of the gang. He thought of the ten thousand dollars. He tightened his grip on his bag and followed her.

Morgan led him forward, navigating the cluttered, dangerous deck with predatory grace, entirely unaffected by the slick surfaces or the heavy sway of the tide. She opened a heavy, watertight steel door near the bow and stepped over the high threshold. Leo scrambled after her, stepping into a cramped, utilitarian office space just aft of the main galley.

The air inside was warm, smelling strongly of old coffee, ozone, and wet wool.

Morgan moved behind a small, bolted-down metal desk. She didn't offer him a seat. She pulled open a drawer and withdrew a thick manila envelope and a clipboard holding a physical copy of the document he had signed digitally on his phone.

"Articles of Employment," Morgan said, tapping the clipboard with a heavy brass pen. "You signed the digital copy. Now you sign the hard copy for the ship's ledger. Then, you get paid."

She tossed the thick envelope onto the desk. It landed with a heavy, muffled thud.

Leo's eyes locked onto the envelope. Ten grand. It was right there. He reached for the pen.

"Read it first," Morgan commanded sharply.

Leo paused, the pen hovering over the paper. He looked down at the dense, legal text. He recognized the shape of the paragraphs from his frantic reading in the boarding house. He skimmed over the top sections—*High-capacity dry storage management*, *caloric prep for Futanari biology*. His eyes skipped right past the biological term again, his brain entirely focused on the cash sitting inches away.

"I read it," Leo said, his voice tight. "I manage the galley. I cook. I clean. I get paid."

Morgan leaned forward, planting her large, calloused hands flat on the desk. The sheer physical presence of her was overwhelming in the small room.

"You didn't read Section IV," she said quietly. "The 'Put it Right' Addendum."

Leo felt a cold sweat break out on the back of his neck. He remembered the words. *Free Use Agreement. Physical Enforcement.* He had convinced himself it was a joke. A hazing ritual.

"I read it," he lied, keeping his eyes on the paper.

"Look at me," Morgan ordered.

Leo slowly raised his eyes.

"This is an F-Class vessel, Leo," Morgan said, her dark eyes locking onto his. "We operate outside standard mainland maritime labor laws. The work we do requires absolute cohesion. Absolute discipline. We do not have the time, the space, or the patience for insubordination, mistakes, or attitude. If you burn a meal, you owe the crew. If you get in the way on deck, you owe the crew. If you talk back to me again like you did on the dock, you owe the crew."

She paused, letting the silence stretch until it was agonizing.

"And out here, in deep water, you don't pay off debt with money. You 'put it right' with your body. You submit to physical enforcement from any superior rank you have offended. You become a resource for aggressive downtime. Do you understand what I am telling you, little boy?"

Leo’s breath hitched. He wanted to scream that it was illegal, that they couldn't do this. But the heavy thud of the boots in his boarding house echoed in his memory. The gang would kill him. These women... they were just talking tough to scare the new guy. They had to be.

"I understand," Leo whispered.

"Good," Morgan said, her expression unchanging. "Sign it."

Leo pressed the pen to the paper and signed his name.

Morgan snatched the clipboard away immediately, signing her own name beneath his with sharp, aggressive strokes as the witness. She picked up the envelope and held it out. Leo reached for it, his fingers brushing the thick paper.

Morgan didn't let go.

"One more thing," she said, her grip on the envelope iron-clad. "Safety protocols."

Leo pulled, but the envelope didn't move. He looked up at her, confused and slightly panicked.

"The engine room and the aft deck hit a hundred and twenty decibels during a haul," Morgan explained, her voice steady. "If you scream, no one will hear you. If you say a safeword, no one will hear you. If you are being physically corrected by a superior and you reach your absolute limit, you use the Visual Safeword."

She let go of the envelope with one hand and reached out, tapping Leo twice, hard, on his right shoulder. The impacts were heavy, precise.

"An unbroken double-tap to the dominant shoulder of the crew member handling you," Morgan instructed. "It halts the correction. Immediately. No questions asked. But know this: using it logs a failure in the ledger, and the debt rolls over with interest."

Leo stared at his shoulder where she had tapped him, a sickening knot forming in his stomach. The absolute seriousness in her tone, the sheer institutional formality of establishing a safeword for physical abuse, began to crack his illusion that this was just a hazing ritual.

"Show me," Morgan demanded.

Leo hesitated.

"Show me the safeword, Leo, or you don't get the money."

Slowly, feeling deeply humiliated and terrified, Leo reached out with a trembling hand and tapped Morgan twice on her right shoulder.

"Adequate," she said. She finally let go of the envelope.

Leo stumbled back slightly, clutching the money to his chest. He had it. He just needed to get back to the deck, pretend he was getting something from his car, and run.

"Grab your bag," Morgan ordered, turning toward an interior heavy steel hatch. "I'll show you your bunk. You need to prep the galley immediately."

"Actually," Leo started, backing toward the exterior door leading to the deck. "I need to... I forgot a bag in my buddy's truck. I just need to run back up the gangway—"

A massive, bone-shaking roar cut him off.

The deck beneath him heaved as the main 50 KW John Deere generator fully engaged. The sound was deafening, a physical pressure that pushed the air out of his lungs. He looked out the small porthole window in the office door.

The heavy wooden gangway was already gone, hauled away by a dock crane. The gap between the rusted steel hull of the *Maiden's Mast* and the concrete pier was widening rapidly. White water churned fiercely at the stern.

They were already moving.

Panic, pure and primal, seized Leo's chest. He dropped his bag. He lunged for the door, his hand grabbing the heavy steel latch.

Morgan didn't try to stop him. She simply watched him, leaning casually against the desk, her arms crossed over her chest.

Leo wrenched the door open and stepped out into the freezing wind. The dock was twenty feet away and receding fast. The dark, freezing water of the harbor churned violently between the ship and the safety of the mainland. He was trapped. There was no bus ride. There was no running away.

"You're a Super-Greenhorn, Leo," Morgan's voice came from right behind him, easily cutting through the roar of the engines. She hadn't shouted, but she was standing close enough that he could feel the heat radiating off her large body. "That means you are the lowest form of life on this vessel. You exist outside the hierarchy until you prove your worth."

Leo slowly turned around, his face pale, the manila envelope crushed in his white-knuckled grip.

Morgan pointed a heavy finger toward the open hatch leading below deck.

"Your bunk is below. You have exactly ten minutes to stow your gear before I expect coffee in the wheelhouse and prep work started in the galley. If you are late, you incur your first debt. Move."

Leo looked at the receding shoreline one last time, the illusion of his clever escape shattering completely. He was trapped on a steel island with a crew that legally owned him, heading out into the deep water.

He swallowed his pride, picked up his duffel bag, and descended into the dark, vibrating belly of the ship.
